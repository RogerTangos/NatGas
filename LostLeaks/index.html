Welcome to LostLeaks

What do you mean they are lost?

What does it means for a leak to be lost?

A "Lost" leak is one that existed in 2014, but wasn't repaired. In 2015, it was "lost" from the public record. Utility companies in Massachusetts have had to report natural gas leaks and repairs since 2014.

  • Records for 2014 and 2015 can be looked up at the department of public utilities

We analyzed the data, matching repairs to leaks. Between 2014 and 2015, it appears that National Grid and Eversource deleted XX% and XX% (respectively) of their leaks, without repairing them.

Why? Some very small percentage of this is likely due to 3rd party repairs - for instance, when a construction company party damages a pipeline. Other leaks would have been deleted because of scheduled replacement to pipes. These wouldn't be marked in the system, we expect that replacements would be mapped as dots along main streets where the pipelines were replaced. Instead, the map looks like confetti. XX % of the leaks is a lot of gas. Something is amiss.

What are Natural Gas Leaks?

Why does this happen?

Natural gas is dangerous. In Boston, natural gas is often transported in old and leak prone cast iron, wrought iron, or unprotected steel pipes.

  • Risky Leaks From Gas Mains
    bu.edu
  • A hidden culprit for dead trees
    stateimpact.npr.org
  • Why are natural gas leaks a problem?
    edf.org/leaks-problem
  • Boston: Snapshot of natural gas leaks
    edf.org/city-snapshots

Why do we care about this?

Contributes to greenhouse gas buildup. Natural gas leaking from the local distribution system accounts for 60 percent to 100 percent of the region’s emissions of methane, one of the most insidious heat-trapping greenhouse gases.

Roughly 1/6 of leaks are potentially explosive, and natural gas is known to kill trees and shrubs, and the methane and carbon monoxide present in natural gas well known to be toxic.

  • Gas infrastructure releasing methane
    seas.harvard.edu

Who pays to repair these leaks?

Aging natural gas distribution system short-changes customers. The Boston region loses 15 billion cubic feet of natural gas, worth $90 million dollars every year. This is 2.7 percent of gas brought into the region.

The cost is not paid by utilities. Instead, utilities, which have local monopolies, pass it on to their customers.

Clearly, utilities have little economic incentive to fix these leaks on their own.

A Data Story

The Process of How we find Lost Leaks.

[1st]

Get data from the State of MA
Energy and Environmental Affairs

Find records via docket number and download from public utilities The data is usually somewhat irregular, and in pdf format.

[2nd]

Parse PDF's using SmallPDF.com, and PDFTables (Audrey at HEETMA)

Audrey turns the pdfs into excel spreadsheets and cleans the excel data by hand. _link a converted spreadsheet_ (Audrey at HEETMA)

[3rd]

Convert data to CSV, and standardize

Albert Carter converted excel spreadsheets into CSV and ran through python program to standardize and sanitize data. Geolocate using google maps API. Insert into DataHub. the csv files

[4th]

Identify Missing Leaks

  • Take list of 2014 Leaks and 2014 Repairs.
  • If a repair happens near an existing leak (approx. 100 ft), count it as repaired.
  • Remaining leaks are counted as un-repaired.
  • If the same leak appears in 2015, it's not missing. Remove it.
  • We know leaks are the same because the date and address are the same.
  • Remaining leaks are missing. Link to the raw JSON file.
